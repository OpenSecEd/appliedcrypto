INL1 has 3 parts:

1.  INL1Quiz, a set of P/F quizzes on Canvas for each of the lectures covering
    cryptographic primitives and randomness. The quiz is not proctored and is 
    to be done individually without coordination with others. You must get P on 
    all of them to pass.
2.  INL1Written, graded P/F, toward the end of the course, where you
    get to apply your knowledge. You will have a week to work on your
    solution (the task itself will take about half a day or a day at
    most.) You may work in pairs on this assignment.
3.  **INL1Oral, graded P/F, at the end of the course (you'll pick one of
    the offered times and sign up), where you get about 10 minutes to
    answer some questions about your solution. There you can also
    clarify anything we found unclear in INL1Written, thus potentially
    getting the preliminary grade from that part improved. If you worked in 
    pairs on INL1Written, you book in pairs for INL1Oral as well.**


## Participating in INL1Oral

To participate in INL1Oral you must book a time slot. Once time slots are
available, you can book a time by going to the [Canvas 
Calendar](https://canvas.kth.se/calendar){.inline_disabled target="_blank"} and 
click "Find meeting", or check the last two weeks of the period.
When that happens, you'll also get an email notification from Canvas that times 
are available (provided you haven't turned those notifications off). In that 
notification there is a link that will bring you to the booking too.
 

### What to expect

Even though the time slots on Canvas show 30 minutes, the expected duration is 
ca. 10 minutes. (We reserve the last 20 minutes for reading the next 
submission.) You\'ll get these questions:

1.  Now that you've done a peer review of another student's solution
    and had some more time to reflect, are there any changes you'd make
    to your solution?
2.  We'll point out relevant/significant mistakes, flaws, or
    ambiguities from your written submission, if there are any, and
    you'll get a chance to explain.
3.  We'll ask you about how the system is bootstrapped, i.e., how do
    you ensure that those authorized to use the system can do so and
    that those that aren't authorized cannot, for example who needs to
    know what, how do they get that knowledge, how do you know you're
    talking to the right entity, how do new entities join the system.

If it's not a clear passing grade, Daniel and Sonja will discuss the
case together and give you an appropriate compensation assignment for
the identified shortcomings.
